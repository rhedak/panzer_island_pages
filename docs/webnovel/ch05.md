# Chapter 5: The Crossing

*Published June 24, 2026*

*Revision 3, updated June 26, 2026*

![The team at the bridge approach, Drona standing on the far ridge watching them](../assets/webnovel/ch05.webp){ .chapter-illustration }

The trail north of the relay cut through stone-littered ground, the scrub still parted cleanly down the center by Drona's hand. Wider sight lines opened ahead, the ridge rising sharp against the failing light. The relay dropped behind us as the ground rose, the interior opening into terrain we had not yet crossed.

Nadeshiko's voice carried down from above. "Bridge ahead. River crossing. Heavy infrastructure on the far bank. This wasn't built for foot traffic. It was built for regular use."

"The approach lanes are recent," Katyusha added. "The rest of the structure is older."

The lanes had guided us since the south coast, through the interior corridors, past the farmhouse with its waiting boots, all the way to the relay. Someone had been clearing a path for us the entire time.

"She was expecting us," Nadeshiko said quietly.

"She has been," I replied.

The lanes. Everything since the south coast had been lane work: the interior corridors, the path past the farmhouse, the approach to the relay. Each section maintained and cleared and open at the center. We had been on the same path since the beginning, and none of us had said it that way yet. She had been clearing it for us the whole time.

We moved north. The relay's light dropped behind the ridge, and the interior closed around us.

---

The break-line formation was the densest we had encountered.

The drones here were calibrated above anything in the interior: heavier units, tighter spacing, overlapping fields that left no approach angle uncovered from at least two positions. Where the interior formations had been guidance geometry, coverage designed to channel movement, this was built to stop movement entirely. Built to hold the approach from the north as well as the south. Different doctrine. Different intention.

Katyusha stopped before the first position.

"A fragment surfaced," she said. "The word 'Doctor.' I was trained to use it as a title, for someone I protected."

A pause.

"There were two of them. I do not remember either name."

"There were two of them," I said, the words surfacing before I reached for them. I did not reach into the space the echo opened.

"Hold that." Maria was already repositioning east. "I can take the opening volley from the east position, Doc."

Katyusha was already reading the formation. She had placed the fragment on a shelf and she did not reach back to it. We went through the first position.

The clearance took twenty minutes. Katyusha worked the approach angles. Nadeshiko threaded the aerial contacts, her circuits between the ground units and the overhead clusters faster than I could follow from the path. Maria closed from the east and worked north up the water channel alongside the bridge approach. Two positions, then a third.

When the third fell, the ridge was ahead of us.

Drona was on it. She had been there since before we started.

"She watched the whole engagement," Katyusha reported.

---

We came to the bridge at the ridge's base.

The far bank was higher ground, the crossing spanning a river channel that was wider here than anything we had forded since the south coast. Beyond the far bank the approach continued north toward the ferry structure with its docks, loading infrastructure, and open water stretching past the dock.

On the far ridge, Drona stood.

She turned. Not to the group: to face me, specifically, and she held there.

A long moment. Her on the ridge, us at the bridge approach, the distance too great to read her expression. But her stillness was readable: the quality of someone who has waited at a fixed point and has seen what they were waiting for arrive.

Maria came up beside me.

"She's not here for the ferry."

I had already reached the same conclusion. The ferry was the point she had built toward. Not the destination. Everything since the south coast, the lanes and the relay and the cleared approach, had been built to move us north to this.

"The ferry is the test."

"Do we still go?" Nadeshiko asked.

"We have to go."

The bridge held us for a moment. Then we crossed.

---

Three positions on the far bank.

Katyusha named them before we reached the first: outer wall, kill corridor, ferry point. Hold full capacity for the ferry point.

The outer wall was drones in coverage arcs over a prepared approach. We came at it from the south. We went through it.

The kill corridor was guard towers in overlapping arcs, cruisers on the north water lane, patrol units between them. Nadeshiko took the towers from altitude. Maria ran the north water lane and cleared it before the cruisers could coordinate. Katyusha pushed the center.

When it cleared, the ferry point was ahead. Water beyond it, open, the far side of the crossing, whatever came after this side of the island.

"Remaining capacity is sufficient," Katyusha reported.

"We go."

---

There was movement at the ferry approach: a single contact.

"NEXUS LIGHT," Katyusha read. "New designation. The pattern is incomplete. Not a full deployment."

Drona was walking to the dock. She moved without urgency, her cardigan shifting slightly in the evening air.

NEXUS LIGHT was smaller than I expected: a compact unit, built for speed and reach rather than the coverage weight of the drones we had cleared through the outer wall. She mounted it from the south approach and it came alive under her hands, the running lights coming up in sequence. When initialization completed, the NEXUS did not settle into a ready stance. It stayed still and read the field before it moved. The headphone lights, which I had always read as teal at distance, shifted to orange.

She looked at us.

Something in her expression moved. Not warmth, not threat. A corner of the mouth. Something that looked like arriving at a destination that had been decided a long time in advance.

"You do not remember me yet," she said. Her voice was lower than I had expected, and more certain. "You will."

She engaged.

"She wanted us here," I said, to no one. We were already moving.

---

*Katyusha*

NEXUS LIGHT opened on a vector I had not shown in this engagement. I adjusted.

It moved to where the adjustment created an opening. Not a recalibration pattern. An anticipation.

I pulled wide to a second position, repositioning for a different angle. The NEXUS was already there before I settled into it, which meant it was running projected behavior, not response to observed movement. I tried to read the protocol. The classification resolved to adaptive targeting and stopped there. The designation I reached for next terminated at the same location. I reached for it again. It terminated again.

It fought like something that knew how we fought.

I knew this doctrine. The specific name did not come.

I moved to an angle I had not used since before the relay approach. The NEXUS adjusted a half-beat late. I used the gap and called the east margin for Maria.

She was already there.

Nadeshiko broke her arc early and held instead of closing. The NEXUS tracked Nadeshiko rather than the flank she left open. It expected Maria on that flank. Maria had not held that flank in this engagement. The NEXUS was reading what it had been told to expect, not what it had seen.

I took the north channel approach while it adjusted for Nadeshiko's deviation. Maria closed from the south. The NEXUS held as long as it could.

It withdrew to the far bank. I noted the withdrawal vector north and moved to the bank.

Erika was at the south approach, looking north after the direction Drona had taken. The sound of the engagement faded into the interior quiet.

---

*Erika*

The water in the channel settled. The smell of scorched metal lingered, and underneath it something else: the astringent note I had been catching since the ridge, stronger now.

Maria was already climbing the slope behind the dock. "Lookout structure up there. Old construction. I want to see what she was really guarding."

I looked at the path Drona had taken north. Already out of sight.

"She was guarding the view."

I followed.

The lookout was old construction, fixed into the slope before any of what we had walked through, before the drones and the lanes and the relay and whatever had happened to empty this island. My hands found the railing at the top before I had reached for it, the metal cold and pitted in exactly the way I expected.

The view north and west stopped everything.

What it showed us was color. And smell: the astringent note concentrated here, chemical and wrong, arriving from the north slope on whatever passed for wind in the flat evening.

A uniform yellow-grey stain stretched to the horizon, the color of vegetation that had died under something deliberate. The color of a process applied. Settlement clusters dotted the affected zone, their outlines still visible beneath the discoloration.

Villages. Twenty thousand people. Ferry distance to the outer islands. I had thought about them once, at the farmhouse, in a specific register, and then stopped and gone back to the work.

There were villages under that color.

*...promise me.*

Not a sound. The shaped absence of one. The fragment surfaced and stopped, as it always stopped, in the same place. Whatever had been promised, I was standing at the edge of the result of it, looking at the full scale of what had been done, and I could not reach the occasion from this side of it.

"The south coast was the only clean corner," Maria said quietly. She did not adjust her hat. "And we just left it."

Nadeshiko turned to me, her usual energy gone. "Erika. The three of us can cross that." She stopped. Restarted. "You should not be able to. Not on foot. Not through that."

"I will be fine."

Katyusha's voice was careful. "The data does not support that conclusion, Doctor."

"I know." I looked at the ruined horizon. "I cannot account for it yet."

The contamination had not touched me. Two days on this island, and it had not touched me.

I had to know what I was not letting myself remember. The answer was north, underneath that color, waiting for me to stop running from it.

"After him anyway," I said. "Drona's trail continues north. We follow it."

"That is what she wants," Nadeshiko said.

"Yes."

I found the path down from the platform.

As I descended the resolve settled: I would not stop until I understood what I had caused.

The team was already moving before I reached the bottom. The island opened north, contaminated, deliberate, waiting, and we went into it.
