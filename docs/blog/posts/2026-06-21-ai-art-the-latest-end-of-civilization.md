# AI art: the latest end of civilization

Oh, for the love of Renaissance paint fumes, here we go again.

Listen up, noble guardians of the sacred brush, you valiant sentinels of hand-crafted pixels and oil on canvas earned through 47 hours of suffering and mild existential dread. How dare a solo developer use generative AI to create character portraits for their reactive turn-based strategy game? Don't they realize true art demands carpal tunnel, unpaid commissions, and the quiet knowledge that your portfolio will be seen by roughly twelve people before the heat death of the universe?

<!-- more -->

Think of the artists! The delicate souls who have toiled since humanity first smeared pigment on cave walls. First photography was the villain: "That's not art, it's just pointing a machine!" Then digital tablets: "Cheating! Real artists bleed ink!" Now AI arrives and we are clutching pearls like Victorian widows spotting a scandalous bicycle. "It's stealing jobs!" proclaim the same people who gleefully use spellcheck, auto-tune, stock assets, 3D modeling software, and Google for references. But this tool? This one crosses the sacred line.

The hypocrisy is chef's kiss perfection. These are the folks who cheered when Photoshop democratized retouching, when Unity let non-coders build games, and when cameras freed us from hiring portrait painters. But AI? It is no mere tool. It is a demon summoned from the silicon pits, trained on the unsolicited scraps of every DeviantArt upload since 2005. Never mind that humans have been stealing styles, compositions, and techniques from each other since one caveman glanced at another's slightly better mammoth drawing and muttered, "Mine is getting better tusks."

And the economic outrage! "It is taking jobs!" Yes, exactly like the automobile took jobs from horse breeders and refrigeration murdered the ice delivery industry. Technological shifts are bloodsports. The market does not owe anyone a monopoly on drawing dramatic anime girls with suspiciously large eyes. Adapt, or become the person still hand-carving stone tablets while the world moves on.

The real comedy peaks when critics demand ethical AI trained only on public domain or opt-in data. Fantastic. Go build it, fund it, and maintain the servers. The rest of us will be over here actually finishing games, writing stories, and composing music at a pace humanity has never seen before. Because that is the dirty secret: AI does not kill art. It explodes the volume of it. Yes, there is noise. But the signal, the genuinely great stuff, grows too.

So spare me the moral grandstanding. If your entire identity hinges on gatekeeping the creative process, maybe the issue is not the technology. Maybe it is time to stop romanticizing the starving artist trope and start wielding the new tools like everyone else who wants to create.

At the end of the day, I am just one guy making Panzer Island, a game with reactive combat, skill trees, and a story about AI units on a mysterious island. Generative AI helped with portraits, code suggestions, and playtesting so I could actually ship something instead of spending years on assets alone. The core gameplay, design, and vision? That is still very human.

If you enjoy the game (or even the rant), feel free to share it. The future of indie dev is not about purity tests. It is about building cool things however you can.

Now if you will excuse me, I have some prompts to refine.
